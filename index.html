Different types of protien powder

Whey Protien

whey protien a dairy product derived from cow’s milk, is probably the most common type of protein you will see, and for good reason. Whey is generally regarded as the highest-quality protein source due to its favorable amino acid content and easy absorption. It can sometimes be added to food products, but you’ll commonly see it on its own as a powder supplement

Whey Concentrate

There are several subtypes of whey protein. Whey concentrate is generally regarded as the lowest-quality option due to its limited refinement process. Concentrates contain up to 80% pure protein by weight, but some products tend to run even lower. The remainder of the nutritional composition is made up of carbohydrates and fat.

Whey Isolate

For a product to be considered a whey isolate, it needs to contain at minimum 90% protein by weight. This extensive refinement and concentrated purity makes whey isolate the de-facto protein powder source for most athletes. Isolates will also come with a lower caloric content than most other options.

Whey Hydrolysate

Whey protein hydrolysate is another variation of whey protein where the product has been treated with either heat, enzymes, or acids to “pre-digest” the product by breaking some of the peptide bonds. This results in a product that is very rapidly digested and typically easily tolerated by most athletes.

Plant-based Protien

Believe it or not, you can actually get a good bit of protein without ever touching a whey product. Plant-based protein powders are generally considered of lower quality, since they are “incomplete,” missing out on at least some of the nine essential aminos. Many plant-based products also have a lower PDCAAS score, indicative of poorer digestibility.

Egg-Protien

Egg-based protein powders are usually more obscure than the other more mainstream options. Egg powders are also more expensive than whey, but if you can find an egg product, it might be a suitable option if you have a milk allergy or simply want to sample a new product for fun.

Casein

Casein is the other type of protein found in cow’s milk and is typically used for making cheese. It has been widely used by bodybuilders before bedtime for its slow digestive properties. Casein curdles in the stomach’s acidic environment, causing it to digest much slower than whey protein.

::::: What to consider before buying a protien powder :::::

Purity

Unfortunately, some protein powders don’t always contain the full amount of protein listed on the label. Moreover, there are occasional incidences of “amino spiking,” a practice that involves adding cheaper amino acids to the nutritional profile to artificially inflate the values on the label.

Nutritional Profile

Ideally, you want a protein powder that contains all of the essential amino acids, making it a complete protein. You also want it to be from an easily-digestible source and to contain a good amount of leucine. Along with this, you should consider the total calorie content of the product and look for a powder that gives you a good amount of protein without the extra fluff.

Pricing

The price of the product is as relevant as any other factor, especially if you’re on a budget. Opting for a whey isolate is a smart choice from a nutritional perspective, but may bruise your wallet more than the other options. If dollar value is of high importance to you, you may want to look towards a whey concentrate. You’ll still get high-quality protein, but it won’t break the bank as much.
